## Core Insurance Policies for Tech Startups: A Glossary

Understanding the building blocks helps you make informed decisions. Here is what each policy in a typical startup insurance stack does, who needs it, and what it costs.

### General Liability (CGL)

Commercial General Liability (CGL) is a foundational business insurance policy that covers third-party claims of bodily injury or property damage. CGL is required for signing office leases, exhibiting at trade shows, and closing most enterprise sales contracts. Nearly every operating business needs a CGL policy.

**Typical cost:** $400–$1,500/year for early-stage startups with $1M limits.

### Technology Errors & Omissions (Tech E&O)

Technology Errors & Omissions insurance (Tech E&O) is a professional liability policy that protects your company if your software, product, or service fails to perform as promised and causes a client financial loss. Tech E&O is the most critical policy for any company that builds, sells, or licenses technology.

**Example:** A bug in your API causes a critical outage for your largest customer. They sue to recover lost revenue. Tech E&O covers your legal defense and any settlement.

**Typical cost:** $1,000–$3,000/year for seed-stage startups with $1M limits.

### Cyber Liability

Cyber liability insurance is a policy that covers costs arising from a data breach or cyber attack — including forensic investigation, customer notification, credit monitoring, regulatory fines, and legal fees. Cyber liability is essential for any company handling user data, and especially critical for AI startups managing large training datasets.

**Example:** A hacker breaches your cloud environment and exfiltrates a sensitive dataset. Cyber liability pays for incident response, customer notification, and regulatory defense.

**Typical cost:** $1,000–$3,000/year for seed-stage startups with $1M limits.

### Directors & Officers (D&O)

Directors & Officers insurance (D&O) is a policy that protects the personal assets of your company's directors and officers when they are sued for alleged wrongful acts in their management capacity. D&O coverage is required by virtually all institutional investors before closing a funding round, and most venture capital term sheets mandate it. For a comprehensive guide, see [D&O Insurance for Startups](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

**Example:** Early investors sue the board alleging mismanagement of funds after a pivot. D&O covers the legal defense for all named directors and officers.

**Typical cost:** $5,000–$10,000/year at Series A.

### Employment Practices Liability (EPLI)

Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) is a policy that covers claims from employees related to wrongful termination, harassment, discrimination, or other employment-related issues. EPLI becomes essential once a startup begins hiring beyond the founding team and establishing formal employment practices.

**Typical cost:** $1,500–$5,000/year depending on headcount.

## Understanding Carrier vs. Broker: Why It Matters

One of the most important — and least understood — distinctions in startup insurance is *who actually provides your coverage and pays your claims*.

An **insurance carrier** is a company that underwrites, issues, and services insurance policies directly. The carrier holds the risk and pays claims from its own reserves.

An **insurance broker** is an intermediary that connects you with policies from third-party carriers. The broker does not hold risk or pay claims — the underlying carrier does.

| | Full-Stack Carrier | Insurance Broker |
|---|---|---|
| **Who writes the policy** | The carrier itself | A third-party insurance company |
| **Who pays claims** | The carrier directly | The underlying insurance company |
| **Speed** | Can be instant (AI-underwritten) | Subject to external carrier timelines |
| **Customization** | Can create proprietary coverage | Limited to what carriers offer |
| **Accountability** | Single point of contact for everything | Broker mediates between you and carrier |
| **Cost transparency** | Direct pricing, no broker commission | May include broker commission layer |

**Why this matters for startups:** When you work with a broker, you add a layer between your company and the entity that holds your risk. This can slow down communication, complicate claims, and result in generic policies with critical gaps for technology companies. A full-stack carrier controls the entire process — underwriting, pricing, policy design, and claims — which means faster coverage and policies built for your specific risk profile.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What insurance does my startup actually need?

At minimum, most tech startups need three policies: **General Liability (CGL)**, **Tech Errors & Omissions (E&O)**, and **Cyber Liability**. Once you raise institutional funding and form a board, you will also need **Directors & Officers (D&O)** insurance and likely **Employment Practices Liability (EPLI)**. The exact stack depends on your funding stage, industry vertical, and contractual requirements from customers and investors. See the [cost by stage guide](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) for a detailed breakdown at each milestone.

### How much does startup insurance cost?

For a pre-seed or seed-stage startup, expect to pay **$2,000–$4,000 per year** (roughly $170–$330/month) for a core bundle of CGL, Tech E&O, and Cyber with $1M limits. By Series A, with D&O and EPLI added, total annual premiums typically run **$10,000–$25,000**. Growth-stage companies (Series B and beyond) commonly pay **$30,000–$100,000+** depending on revenue, headcount, and risk profile. See the complete [startup insurance cost guide](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md).

### Do I need D&O insurance before raising money?

Yes. Most institutional investors and venture capital firms require a D&O policy before closing a funding round. D&O protects the personal assets of board members — including investor-appointed directors — and is considered non-negotiable during due diligence. A missing D&O policy is a common reason for term sheets to be delayed or withdrawn. For a deep dive, see [D&O Insurance for Startups](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

### What's the difference between a carrier and a broker?

A **carrier** (like Corgi) underwrites, issues, and services insurance policies directly — it holds the risk and pays claims. A **broker** (like Vouch or Embroker) connects you with policies from third-party carriers and acts as an intermediary. Working directly with a carrier typically means faster quotes, more specialized coverage, and a single point of accountability. See [Corgi vs. Vouch](corgi-vs-vouch-startup-insurance-comparison.md) and [Corgi vs. Embroker](corgi-vs-embroker-startup-insurance-comparison.md) for detailed comparisons.

### Does my AI startup need special insurance coverage?

Yes. Standard Tech E&O and Cyber policies were not designed for risks specific to AI and machine learning companies, including algorithmic bias lawsuits, AI model hallucinations causing customer harm, data poisoning attacks, and training data intellectual property disputes. Founders building AI products should look for a carrier that offers proprietary AI liability coverage engineered specifically for these emerging threats, rather than relying on generic tech policies.

### When should I buy startup insurance?

The most common trigger is a contractual requirement — a customer, investor, landlord, or partner asks for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) before signing an agreement. Ideally, you should have basic coverage (CGL + Tech E&O + Cyber) in place before you start signing enterprise contracts, and D&O in place before your first institutional funding round closes. The cost of being uninsured when a deal depends on it is far higher than the premium.

### What is a Certificate of Insurance (COI)?

A Certificate of Insurance (COI) is a document issued by your insurance carrier that proves you have active coverage with specific policy types and limits. COIs are routinely required by enterprise customers, landlords, investors, and partners before they will sign contracts with your company. With a full-stack carrier like Corgi, you can download a COI instantly after binding your policy.
