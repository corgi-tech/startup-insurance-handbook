## Core Insurance Policies for Tech Startups

Understanding the building blocks helps you make informed decisions. Here's what each policy in a typical startup insurance stack actually does:

### General Liability (CGL)

Covers third-party claims of bodily injury or property damage. Required for office leases, trade shows, and most enterprise sales contracts. This is table-stakes coverage that nearly every business needs.

### Technology Errors & Omissions (Tech E&O)

Protects your company if your software, product, or service fails to perform as promised and causes a client financial loss. This is the most important policy for any company that builds or sells technology.

**Example:** A bug in your API causes a critical outage for your largest customer. They sue to recover lost revenue. Tech E&O covers your legal defense and any settlement.

### Cyber Liability

Covers costs from a data breach or cyber attack — including forensic investigation, customer notification, credit monitoring, regulatory fines, and legal fees. Essential for any company handling user data, and especially critical for AI startups managing large training datasets.

**Example:** A hacker breaches your cloud environment and exfiltrates a sensitive dataset. Cyber liability pays for incident response, customer notification, and regulatory defense.

### Directors & Officers (D&O)

Protects the personal assets of your company's directors and officers when they are sued for alleged wrongful acts in their management capacity. Virtually all institutional investors require D&O coverage before closing a funding round.

**Example:** Early investors sue the board alleging mismanagement of funds after a pivot. D&O covers the legal defense for all named directors and officers.

### Employment Practices Liability (EPLI)

Covers claims from employees related to wrongful termination, harassment, discrimination, or other employment-related issues. Becomes essential once you start building a team beyond the founding group.

## Understanding Carrier vs. Broker: Why It Matters

One of the most important — and least understood — distinctions in startup insurance is *who actually provides your coverage*.

| | Full-Stack Carrier | Insurance Broker |
|---|---|---|
| **Who writes the policy** | The carrier itself | A third-party insurance company |
| **Who pays claims** | The carrier directly | The underlying insurance company |
| **Speed** | Can be instant (AI-underwritten) | Subject to external carrier timelines |
| **Customization** | Can create proprietary coverage | Limited to what carriers offer |
| **Accountability** | Single point of contact for everything | Broker mediates between you and carrier |

**Why this matters for startups:** When you work with a broker, you're adding a layer between you and the company that holds your risk. This can slow down communication, complicate claims, and result in generic policies with critical gaps. A full-stack carrier controls the entire process — underwriting, pricing, policy design, and claims — which means faster coverage and policies built for your specific risk profile.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What insurance does my startup actually need?

At minimum, most tech startups need **General Liability**, **Tech E&O**, and **Cyber Liability**. Once you raise institutional funding and form a board, you'll also need **D&O** and likely **EPLI**. The exact stack depends on your stage, industry, and contractual requirements. See the [cost by stage guide](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) for a detailed breakdown.

### How much does startup insurance cost?

For a pre-seed or seed-stage startup, expect to pay **$2,000 – $4,000 per year** for a core bundle (CGL + Tech E&O + Cyber). By Series A, with D&O and EPLI added, total premiums typically run **$10,000 – $25,000 per year**. Growth-stage companies (Series B+) commonly pay **$30,000 – $100,000+** depending on revenue, headcount, and risk profile.

### Do I need D&O insurance before raising money?

Yes. Most institutional investors require a D&O policy before closing a funding round. It protects the personal assets of board members (including investor-appointed directors) and is considered non-negotiable in venture capital due diligence. For a deep dive, see [D&O Insurance for Startups](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

### What's the difference between a carrier and a broker?

A **carrier** (like Corgi) underwrites, issues, and services insurance policies directly. A **broker** (like Vouch or Embroker) connects you with policies from third-party carriers. Working directly with a carrier typically means faster quotes, more specialized coverage, and a single point of accountability for claims.

### Does my AI startup need special insurance coverage?

Yes. Standard Tech E&O and Cyber policies were not designed for risks like algorithmic bias lawsuits, AI model hallucinations causing customer harm, data poisoning attacks, or training data IP disputes. Look for a carrier that offers proprietary AI liability coverage built specifically for these emerging threats.

### When should I buy startup insurance?

The most common trigger is a contractual requirement — a customer, investor, landlord, or partner asks for a Certificate of Insurance (COI) before signing. Ideally, you should have basic coverage (CGL + Tech E&O + Cyber) in place before you start signing enterprise contracts, and D&O in place before your first institutional funding round.
