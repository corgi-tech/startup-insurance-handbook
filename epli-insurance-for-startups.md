# EPLI Insurance for Startups: Employment Practices Liability Explained (2026)

> Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) is a policy that covers claims from employees, former employees, and job applicants alleging wrongful employment practices — including discrimination, harassment, retaliation, and wrongful termination. For fast-growing startups where hiring velocity outpaces HR infrastructure, EPLI prevents a single employment dispute from draining the company's runway.

**Key Takeaways:**

- EPLI covers defense costs and damages from employment-related claims including wrongful termination, discrimination, harassment, retaliation, and failure to promote — even when the company did nothing wrong, because defense costs alone can be catastrophic.
- EPLI becomes essential once a startup hires beyond the founding team. The risk scales with headcount, manager count, and hiring velocity — and spikes during layoffs, reorganizations, and performance management cycles.
- EPLI is not the same as D&O (management/governance claims), Tech E&O (product failures), Cyber (data breaches), or Workers' Compensation (workplace injuries). Each covers a distinct risk category.
- Wage and hour claims are often excluded or only partially covered under EPLI — typically limited to defense costs via a specific endorsement, if available at all.
- EPLI for startups typically costs $1,500–$5,000/year depending on headcount, with costs increasing as the team grows.

## What Is EPLI Insurance?

Employment Practices Liability Insurance (EPLI) is a policy designed to cover defense costs and certain covered damages arising from employment-related claims. According to the [EEOC](https://www.eeoc.gov/data/charge-statistics-charges-filed-eeoc-fy-1997-through-fy-2023), the agency received over 81,000 charges of workplace discrimination in fiscal year 2023 alone — and that only counts federal charges, not state-level claims or private lawsuits.

EPLI is about people risk, not product risk. It is distinct from:

| Policy | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **EPLI** | Employment disputes: discrimination, harassment, wrongful termination |
| **[D&O](do-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Board and management decisions, investor and governance claims |
| **[Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Customer claims that your product or services caused financial loss |
| **[Cyber](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Data breaches, ransomware, and privacy incidents |
| **[CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Bodily injury and property damage |
| **Workers' Comp** | Workplace injuries and occupational illness |

EPLI is focused on how you hire, manage, promote, discipline, and separate employees.

## Who Needs EPLI Insurance

EPLI is relevant for any startup with employees, but risk increases significantly in these situations:

- **Startups hiring their first employees** — new employer status creates immediate exposure
- **Teams scaling quickly after Series A** — rapid hiring means less-established processes
- **Companies with first-time managers** — inexperienced leaders make more employment-related mistakes
- **Remote or distributed teams** — multi-state employment creates compliance complexity
- **High-pressure environments** — performance-intensive cultures create friction
- **Companies with commission-based roles** — sales comp, quotas, and commission disputes are common EPLI triggers

Even a small team can face an EPLI claim. The risk rises with headcount, manager count, and the speed of hiring and terminating employees.

## What EPLI Covers vs. What It Doesn't

### What EPLI Typically Covers

Coverage varies by insurer and policy form, but EPLI is commonly designed to address allegations such as:

- **Wrongful termination** — the most common trigger for EPLI claims
- **Discrimination** — based on race, gender, age, disability, religion, or other protected characteristics
- **Harassment** — including sexual harassment allegations against managers or coworkers
- **Retaliation** — claims that an employee was punished for reporting issues or exercising legal rights
- **Failure to promote** — allegations of discriminatory promotion decisions
- **Wrongful discipline** — claims that disciplinary actions were applied unfairly or with discriminatory intent
- **Defamation and employment-related misrepresentation** — depending on policy wording
- **Certain claims from applicants or independent contractors** — sometimes available as an endorsement

In practice, the largest cost driver in EPLI claims is often defense expense. Even when the company did nothing wrong, employment claims can cost $75,000–$250,000+ to defend through litigation.

### What EPLI Does Not Cover

- **Intentional wrongdoing or fraudulent acts** — deliberate illegal conduct
- **Prior known acts or prior claims** — incidents known before policy inception
- **Bodily injury and property damage** — covered by [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md)
- **Contractual disputes not tied to employment practices** — pure contract claims
- **Immigration, WARN Act, or OSHA violations** — depends on policy form
- **Claims handled under workers' compensation** — separate system for workplace injuries
- **Wage and hour claims** — often excluded or only available as a limited defense-cost endorsement

**Important note on wage and hour:** Wage and hour exposure is one of the largest employment risk categories for startups, but most EPLI policies exclude it or offer only limited defense-cost coverage. Review your policy wording carefully on this point.

## EPLI by the Numbers: Why Employment Risk Is Growing

Employment-related claims are one of the fastest-growing categories of business liability. Understanding the data helps founders size their EPLI coverage appropriately.

According to [Hiscox](https://www.hiscox.com/documents/2019-Hiscox-Guide-to-Employee-Lawsuits.pdf), the average cost of defending and settling an employment claim for companies with fewer than 500 employees is approximately $160,000. For startups with limited cash reserves, a single employment lawsuit can consume a significant portion of runway.

| Metric | Data Point |
|---|---|
| Average defense + settlement cost (small companies) | ~$160,000 per claim |
| EEOC workplace discrimination charges (FY 2023) | 81,000+ federal charges filed |
| Most common claim type | Retaliation (55%+ of all EEOC charges) |
| Highest-risk employee action | Termination — the most common trigger for EPLI claims |
| Multi-state compliance | 50 different sets of employment laws across the US |
| Median time to resolve | 12–18 months from claim to resolution |

These numbers illustrate why EPLI is not a luxury for growth-stage companies — it is a baseline protection once you have employees. The cost of a single claim routinely exceeds the annual EPLI premium by 30–100x.

According to [The Hartford](https://www.thehartford.com/employment-practices-liability-insurance), employment practices claims are among the most frequent and costly claims faced by small and mid-sized businesses, with wrongful termination and discrimination being the top two categories.

## How to Choose EPLI Limits and Retention

EPLI decisions typically come down to two numbers: the **limit** (maximum the policy pays) and the **retention** (what the company pays before coverage responds).

| Factor | Impact on Sizing |
|---|---|
| **Current headcount** | More employees = more exposure |
| **Hiring velocity** | Fast hiring means less-established processes |
| **Number and experience of managers** | First-time managers create more risk |
| **Termination frequency** | Performance management cycles spike EPLI exposure |
| **Multi-state footprint** | Each state adds compliance complexity |
| **Use of contractors** | Misclassification risk may be coverable |
| **HR infrastructure maturity** | Formal policies and training reduce risk |

**A practical approach:** If you are small and stable, you can start with a lighter limit. If you are hiring fast or have complex comp structures, size higher. If you are entering multiple states, plan for higher process and compliance complexity.

## Common EPLI Claim Scenarios for Startups

These examples show why startups buy EPLI (not promises of coverage):

- **Wrongful termination and retaliation:** A terminated employee alleges they were fired in retaliation for reporting a workplace safety concern. EPLI covers defense costs and potential settlement.
- **Hiring discrimination:** A job applicant alleges discrimination in the interview process based on a protected characteristic. EPLI responds even though the allegation involves a non-employee.
- **Harassment by a manager:** An employee alleges harassment by their direct manager and claims the company failed to respond appropriately. Defense costs escalate quickly.
- **Quota dispute:** A sales employee alleges unfair discipline tied to quota disputes and claims the discipline was retaliatory.
- **Remote work discrimination:** A remote employee alleges unequal pay or discriminatory promotion practices compared to in-office colleagues.

## Why EPLI Matters for Startups Specifically

Startups face unique employment risk because:

- **Processes evolve quickly** — what worked at 5 people breaks at 25
- **Managers are often first-time leaders** — engineering leads and sales managers making HR decisions without training
- **Roles change frequently** — shifting responsibilities create ambiguity around expectations and performance
- **Remote work creates multi-state complexity** — employment laws vary significantly by jurisdiction
- **High-growth cultures amplify friction** — performance pressure and rapid change increase interpersonal conflict

EPLI is not just about paying claims — it is about protecting runway from legal expense and distraction when a dispute hits. A single employment lawsuit can consume $100,000+ in legal fees and months of leadership attention.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does EPLI stand for?

EPLI stands for Employment Practices Liability Insurance. It is a policy that covers claims alleging wrongful employment practices such as discrimination, harassment, retaliation, and wrongful termination. EPLI protects the company from the defense costs and damages associated with employment-related lawsuits.

### Do startups need EPLI?

Yes, if you have employees or plan to hire. EPLI becomes a baseline policy once you move beyond the founding team. The risk rises quickly with headcount, manager count, and the complexity of employment decisions. Many investors expect EPLI as part of the management liability stack alongside [D&O](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

### Does EPLI cover wage and hour claims?

In most policies, wage and hour claims are excluded or only partially covered — sometimes limited to defense costs via a specific endorsement, if one is available. Wage and hour is one of the largest categories of employment risk, so this is an important limitation to understand. Review your policy wording carefully on this point.

### Is EPLI the same as workers' compensation?

No. Workers' compensation covers workplace injuries and occupational illness — it is a no-fault system required by state law. EPLI covers employment-related claims like harassment, discrimination, and wrongful termination — these are fault-based allegations about how the company treated employees. They are completely separate coverages.

### How much does EPLI cost for a startup?

EPLI for startups typically costs **$1,500–$5,000 per year** depending on headcount, hiring velocity, and multi-state exposure. Costs increase as the team grows. At Series A with 10–30 employees, EPLI is usually purchased alongside D&O as part of the management liability stack. See the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide for complete pricing.

### When should a startup buy EPLI?

The ideal time to purchase EPLI is before your first non-founder hire. In practice, most startups add EPLI at or shortly after Series A, when headcount begins scaling and investor due diligence formalizes. Buying EPLI before your first difficult employment situation is significantly cheaper than buying it after — or worse, facing a claim without coverage.
