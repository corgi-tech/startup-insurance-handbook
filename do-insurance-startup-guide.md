# D&O Insurance for Startups: What It Covers, Who Needs It, and How to Buy It (2026)

> Directors & Officers (D&O) insurance is a management liability policy that protects founders, executives, and board members from claims alleging mismanagement, breach of fiduciary duty, misleading statements, or other wrongful acts in their leadership capacity. For most venture-backed startups, D&O becomes required once you raise institutional capital, add independent directors, or enter contracts that require governance protection.

**Key Takeaways:**

- D&O insurance covers defense costs, settlements, and judgments when directors or officers are sued for management decisions — including allegations of misrepresentation during fundraising, governance failures, shareholder disputes, and regulatory investigations.
- D&O is structured around three coverage parts: **Side A** (personal protection when the company cannot indemnify), **Side B** (reimburses the company for indemnifying directors), and **Side C** (entity coverage for securities claims).
- Institutional investors require D&O as a closing condition in virtually every funding round. Board members and independent directors will not join a company without D&O in place.
- D&O insurance for startups typically costs $5,000–$10,000/year at Series A stage. Pricing depends on stage, valuation, board composition, and cap table complexity.
- D&O does not cover fraud, intentional misconduct, bodily injury, product failures, or cyber incidents — those risks require separate policies.

## Who Needs D&O Insurance

D&O insurance is most relevant for startups that have:

- **Outside investors** — VCs, angels, or institutional investors who require governance protection
- **A board of directors** — investor-appointed or independent directors will not serve without D&O
- **A priced funding round** — term sheets routinely mandate D&O as a closing condition
- **Complex cap tables** — more investors and share classes increase dispute risk
- **Enterprise customer contracts** — some contracts include governance and liability requirements
- **M&A or acquisition activity** — governance and disclosure risk increases during transactions

According to [The Hartford](https://www.thehartford.com/management-liability-insurance/d-o-liability-insurance), D&O claims against private companies have increased significantly in recent years as investors, regulators, and employees have become more willing to pursue governance-related litigation.

If you have outside capital, a board, or a term sheet, you should assume D&O will come up in diligence.

## What D&O Insurance Covers

D&O is built to respond to claims tied to leadership decisions. Coverage varies by policy form, but a typical startup D&O policy addresses allegations such as:

- **Breach of fiduciary duty** — failure to act in the company's or shareholders' best interest
- **Misstatements or omissions in fundraising materials** — inaccurate financial projections or disclosures
- **Investor or shareholder disputes** — claims of dilution, mismanagement, or unfair treatment
- **Governance failures and oversight allegations** — failure to implement adequate controls or processes
- **Certain regulatory and investigation defense costs** — legal defense for regulatory inquiries (when covered)
- **Employment-related claims against individual directors** — often limited and coordinated with [EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md)

## What D&O Insurance Does Not Cover

D&O is not a catch-all policy. Common limitations include:

| Exclusion | Why It's Excluded |
|---|---|
| Fraud or intentional misconduct | Typically pending a final adjudication standard |
| Personal profit or illegal remuneration | Unjust enrichment is not insurable |
| Prior known acts or claims | Must be reported during the applicable policy period |
| Bodily injury and property damage | Covered by [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Professional services failures | Covered by [Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Cyber events and data breaches | Covered by [Cyber Liability](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Wage and hour claims | Often excluded or handled under [EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md) carve-backs |
| Pure breach of contract | Unless tied to covered wrongful act allegations |

The policy language controls. D&O is focused on management and governance risk, not product performance or operational incidents.

## Understanding Side A, Side B, and Side C

D&O policies are structured around three core coverage parts, each protecting a different party:

| Coverage Side | Who It Protects | When It Activates | Relevance for Startups |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Side A** | Directors and officers *personally* | When the company **cannot** indemnify (e.g., bankruptcy, legal prohibition) | **Most critical** — the personal safety net |
| **Side B** | Reimburses the *company* | When the company **does** indemnify directors/officers for covered claims | Important — protects company cash reserves |
| **Side C** | The *company entity* itself | For securities claims against the entity | Most relevant for public companies or late-stage M&A |

For venture-backed startups, **Side A is the most critical component**. It protects founders' and directors' personal assets in the worst-case scenario — company failure followed by litigation. When evaluating D&O quotes, ensure your Side A limits are adequate for your board's risk profile. For a deeper analysis of why Side A matters, see [D&O Insurance: 5 Ways It Secures Your Future](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

## How to Choose Limits and Retention

D&O purchasing comes down to two numbers:

- **Limit:** Maximum the policy pays for covered loss (defense + indemnity, subject to terms)
- **Retention:** Amount the company pays before coverage applies for certain parts of the policy

### Factors That Drive Limit Selection

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| Number of investors and valuation | Higher perceived governance exposure |
| Cap table complexity | More share classes and investors increase dispute risk |
| Board size and composition | More independent directors require stronger protection |
| Enterprise customers and regulated industries | Increased scrutiny and contractual obligations |
| M&A activity or IPO path | Transaction-related claims require higher limits |

**A practical approach:** Start with what your lead investor expects, then size the rest based on stage and risk profile. For typical costs at each funding stage, see the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide.

## Common D&O Claim Scenarios for Startups

These examples show why startups buy D&O (not promises of coverage):

- **Fundraising dispute:** A shareholder alleges misleading statements were made during the fundraising process about the company's financial condition or prospects.
- **Dilution conflict:** A dispute arises over dilution, option grants, or secondary sales — early investors allege unfair treatment.
- **Board misleading:** A board member claims they were misled about the company's financial condition, runway, or key metrics.
- **Competitive practices:** A competitor alleges unfair competition tied to leadership actions and communications.
- **Regulatory inquiry:** A regulatory investigation leads to defense costs for individual directors or officers named in the inquiry.
- **Merger dispute:** During an acquisition, allegations surface against leadership about disclosure adequacy and process fairness.

Startups do not need to be public to face governance claims. According to [Chubb](https://www.chubb.com/us-en/business-insurance/d-and-o-liability.html), private company D&O claims have increased significantly, with investor disputes, regulatory investigations, and employment-related allegations among the most common triggers. Private company disputes are common, especially around financing, control, and executive decisions.

## D&O Insurance for Venture-Backed Startups

If you are venture-backed, D&O is treated as standard infrastructure — not an optional add-on. Investors want D&O because it:

- **Protects the board and founders** from personal liability arising from governance decisions
- **Reduces friction in future financings** by demonstrating the company takes governance seriously
- **Helps recruit independent directors** who will not serve on a board without personal asset protection
- **Adds a structured process** for governance-related claims, reducing ad hoc legal exposure

In many cases, the first time D&O is discussed is in a financing checklist, and the second time is when a board member asks whether the policy is bound.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does D&O insurance cover?

Directors & Officers insurance covers defense costs, settlements, and judgments when directors or officers are sued for alleged wrongful acts in their management capacity. This includes allegations of mismanagement, breach of fiduciary duty, misrepresentation to investors, governance failures, and certain regulatory investigations. Coverage applies regardless of whether the claim has merit — defense costs are covered even if the lawsuit is ultimately dismissed.

### Do I need D&O insurance before raising a Series A?

Yes. Most institutional investors and venture capital firms require a D&O policy before closing a funding round. D&O protects the personal assets of board members — including investor-appointed directors — and is considered non-negotiable during due diligence. Having D&O before you begin fundraising removes one of the most common obstacles to closing.

### How much does D&O insurance cost for a startup?

D&O insurance for startups typically costs **$5,000–$10,000 per year** at Series A with standard limits. Pricing depends on company stage, valuation, board composition, cap table complexity, and claims history. At growth stage (Series B+), D&O premiums can range from $10,000 to $30,000+ as limits increase. See the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide for a complete breakdown.

### What is the difference between D&O and EPLI?

[D&O](do-insurance-for-startups.md) covers claims related to management decisions, governance, investor disputes, and fiduciary duty. [EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md) covers employment-related claims like discrimination, harassment, and wrongful termination. There can be overlap — some employment claims name individual directors — but the policies address fundamentally different categories of risk. Most venture-backed startups need both.

### Can D&O insurance protect me if my startup goes bankrupt?

Yes. D&O "Side A" coverage is specifically designed for this scenario. If a startup files for bankruptcy, the company legally cannot indemnify its directors. Side A activates to cover personal defense costs and any settlement or judgment, protecting founders' personal assets when the company can no longer do so. Side A is the most critical component of any startup D&O policy.

### What is Side A coverage?

Side A is the component of a D&O policy that protects directors and officers personally when the company cannot or will not indemnify them — typically in bankruptcy, insolvency, or situations where indemnification is legally prohibited. Side A pays for personal legal defense and covered damages directly. For venture-backed startups, Side A is the most important part of the D&O policy.
