# Startup Insurance Guide for Founders: Everything You Need to Know (2026)

> Startup insurance is a set of business insurance policies that protect your company, your leadership team, and your contracts from common claims. Most founders buy insurance for one reason: a customer, investor, or landlord requires proof of coverage. But the best time to set it up is before that forcing event — so sales and fundraising don't stall when a COI is needed.

**Key Takeaways:**

- Startup insurance is a stack of policies, not one policy. The core stack for many tech startups is D&O, Cyber, Tech E&O, and CGL — with EPLI, HNOA, Fiduciary, and Media Liability added as the company grows.
- The three forcing functions that trigger insurance purchases are **customers** (enterprise contracts require COIs), **investors** (term sheets mandate D&O), and **landlords** (leases require CGL).
- The fastest way to lose time is waiting until a contract signature or term sheet close to start the insurance process. Getting coverage in place before you need it prevents deal delays.
- Aligning your insurance with your contract templates is critical — insurance cannot fix an MSA that promises unlimited liability or unrealistic indemnities.
- Common founder mistakes include waiting until a term sheet to buy D&O, buying CGL and assuming it covers product failures (it doesn't — that's Tech E&O), and forgetting HNOA because you don't own vehicles.

## Why Founders Put Off Startup Insurance

Early-stage teams optimize for product, hiring, and runway. Insurance feels like paperwork until it becomes a blocker. The problem is timing: insurance is hardest to buy when you're under deadline pressure from a customer contract, a lease, or an investor checklist.

If you're planning any of these in the next 30 to 90 days, start the insurance process now:

- Signing an enterprise MSA or SOW
- Moving into an office or signing a lease
- Closing a priced round or adding board members
- Hiring managers or growing headcount quickly
- Onboarding an enterprise customer with a security questionnaire

According to [Forbes](https://www.forbes.com/advisor/business-insurance/what-is-business-insurance/), business insurance is one of the most commonly delayed operational tasks for startups — and one of the most disruptive when it becomes urgent.

## The Three Forcing Functions: Customers, Investors, Landlords

### Customers

Enterprise customers often require specific policies and minimum limits in their vendor agreements. The most common requests include:

- [Cyber Liability](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md) — required in security questionnaires and vendor risk reviews
- [Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md) — required in MSAs, SOWs, and SLAs
- [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) — required in vendor onboarding and sometimes in data center or co-location agreements
- A **Certificate of Insurance (COI)** proving all of the above

### Investors and Board Members

Fundraising triggers [D&O](do-insurance-startup-guide.md) because institutional investors and independent directors want personal asset protection. D&O is a closing condition in virtually every institutional funding round. Not having D&O ready can delay a round by weeks.

### Landlords, Venues, and Vendors

Office leases and event venues commonly require [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) proof, and some vendors require it before onboarding. Coworking spaces, conference venues, and trade shows all have insurance requirements.

## How to Avoid Procurement and Contract Delays

### 1. Know the Common Contract Asks

Have a process for:
- **Certificates of Insurance (COI)** — the most common request
- **Additional Insured status** — commonly required on CGL
- **Primary and Non-contributory wording** — ensures your policy responds first
- **Waivers of Subrogation** — sometimes required by landlords and customers

### 2. Align Insurance With Your Contract Templates

Insurance cannot fix an MSA that promises unlimited liability or unrealistic indemnities. Work with legal counsel to align your default contract terms with the coverages you buy. If your MSA promises $10M in indemnity and your policy has a $1M limit, there's a $9M gap no insurance will fill.

### 3. Build a Simple Internal Checklist

Before signing any customer contract, confirm:
- Which policies are required
- Minimum limits by line
- Additional insured requirements
- COI turnaround time

## What Affects Startup Insurance Pricing and Underwriting

Insurance pricing is based on your risk profile. Common drivers include:

| Factor | Impact on Pricing |
|---|---|
| **Stage, revenue, and growth rate** | Higher revenue = higher exposure = higher premiums |
| **Customer profile and contract terms** | SLAs, indemnities, and enterprise customers increase E&O/Cyber risk |
| **Data sensitivity and security controls** | Strong security posture lowers Cyber premiums |
| **Claims history** | Prior claims increase cost; clean history reduces it |
| **Headcount and HR practices** | More employees = higher EPLI exposure |
| **Board structure and cap table** | Complex governance increases D&O risk |
| **Industry and use case** | Fintech, healthcare, and AI may face different risk profiles |

For specific cost ranges at each funding stage, see the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide.

## Common Founder Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's Costly |
|---|---|
| Waiting until a term sheet to start D&O | Delays the round — D&O underwriting takes time |
| Buying CGL and assuming it covers product failures | Product failures are [Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md), not CGL |
| Treating Cyber as a checkbox | Missing key coverage components when a breach occurs |
| Forgetting HNOA because you don't own vehicles | Fails auto liability requirements in enterprise contracts |
| Letting claims-made policies lapse | Prior work becomes uninsured — dangerous gap |
| Underestimating employment risk | EPLI claims are expensive even when the company did nothing wrong |

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What insurance do startups need first?

Most tech startups start with [Cyber](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md), [Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md), and [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) based on customer and contract requirements. [D&O](do-insurance-startup-guide.md) is added as governance matures (typically at or before Series A). [EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md) becomes important once you hire beyond the founding team.

### When do startups need D&O insurance?

Most commonly when you raise institutional capital, add a board, or close a priced round where D&O is a due diligence requirement. Having D&O in place before fundraising begins removes a common obstacle. See the [D&O Guide](do-insurance-startup-guide.md) and the [5 Ways D&O Secures Your Future](do-insurance-for-startups.md) for comprehensive coverage.

### What is the difference between Tech E&O and CGL?

[Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md) covers customer claims that your product or services caused financial loss — professional liability. [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) covers third-party bodily injury and property damage — physical-world claims. A software bug that costs a customer revenue is E&O. A visitor who trips in your office is CGL. Most tech startups need both.

### Do software startups need general liability?

Yes, in most cases. Leases, vendors, events, and enterprise contracts commonly require [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) even if you don't have a physical product. CGL is often the first policy requested in a Certificate of Insurance.

### How much does startup insurance cost?

Pre-seed/seed: $2,000–$4,000/year for a core bundle. Series A: $10,000–$25,000/year with D&O and EPLI. Growth stage: $30,000–$100,000+/year with higher limits. See the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide for a detailed breakdown.

### What is a Certificate of Insurance (COI)?

A COI is a document proving you have active insurance coverage with specific policy types and limits. COIs are routinely required by enterprise customers, landlords, investors, and partners before signing contracts.
