# How Much Does Startup Insurance Cost? A Breakdown by Funding Stage (2026)

> Startup insurance costs range from **$2,000–$4,000/year** at pre-seed to **$30,000–$100,000+/year** at growth stage. The exact cost depends on your funding stage, headcount, revenue, industry vertical, and risk profile. This guide provides a data-backed breakdown at every milestone.

**Key Takeaways:**

- Pre-seed and seed startups typically pay $2,000–$4,000/year (~$170–$330/month) for a core bundle of General Liability, Tech E&O, and Cyber with $1M limits.
- Series A companies add D&O and EPLI, bringing total annual premiums to $10,000–$25,000. D&O insurance alone costs $5,000–$10,000/year at this stage.
- Growth-stage companies (Series B and beyond) pay $30,000–$100,000+/year with higher limits and customized coverage.
- Working with a full-stack carrier instead of a broker can reduce costs by eliminating middleman commissions and providing more accurate, AI-driven risk-based pricing.
- Strong cybersecurity practices, clear customer contracts, and documented internal processes can all materially lower your premiums.

## Why Old Insurance Models Can't Keep Up

For decades, getting business insurance has meant navigating a slow, frustrating process built for a different era. Legacy carriers and modern-looking brokers alike often still rely on intermediary models — placing policies on behalf of third-party insurance companies rather than underwriting directly. This creates three critical problems for startup founders:

- **Delayed Deals:** The quoting and binding process can take days or weeks, delaying your ability to sign customer contracts, close a funding round, or produce a Certificate of Insurance (COI) for a partner or landlord.
- **Irrelevant Coverage:** Generic policies from carriers that primarily serve restaurants, retailers, and construction companies often fail to cover tech-specific risks like algorithmic bias, AI model failures, data breaches from your ML pipeline, or intellectual property disputes related to training data.
- **Dangerous Gaps:** An underwriter who doesn't understand your tech stack or business model can leave you exposed. If your insurance provider doesn't specialize in technology companies, you risk paying for a policy that won't perform when you need it most — especially for emerging risks that traditional policy language doesn't address.

## Startup Insurance Costs: A Stage-by-Stage Breakdown

Your insurance needs evolve as you scale. The coverage that protects a two-person pre-seed startup is fundamentally different from what a 50-person growth-stage company with enterprise customers and a formal board requires. Here is what to expect at each funding milestone.

### Pre-Seed & Seed Stage ($2,000–$4,000/year)

At the earliest stage, your focus is on building your product, landing first customers, and securing initial funding. Contractual requirements are the primary driver of your insurance needs — landlords, customers, investors, and accelerator programs will require proof of basic coverage before they sign agreements with your company.

**Essential Policies:**
- **General Liability (CGL)** — required for office leases, trade shows, and most customer contracts
- **Tech Errors & Omissions (E&O)** — protects against claims that your software failed to perform and caused client harm
- **Cyber Liability** — covers data breach costs, forensic investigation, customer notification, and regulatory fines

**Typical Cost:** For a pre-revenue or early-revenue startup, a comprehensive package with $1M limits for these three core coverages generally costs between **$2,000 and $4,000 per year** (approximately **$170–$330 per month**). Bundling these policies is the most cost-effective approach — purchasing them individually from separate carriers typically costs more and adds administrative complexity.

**What This Unlocks:** This foundational bundle satisfies the insurance requirements in most customer contracts, vendor agreements, office leases, and accelerator programs. It is the key that unlocks your first major deals and partnerships without delay.

### Series A Stage ($10,000–$25,000/year)

After raising a Series A, your risk profile changes significantly. You have more employees, more revenue, more customers, a formal board of directors with investor-appointed members, and higher-value contracts. Your insurance must scale to match. According to [Insureon](https://www.insureon.com/small-business-insurance/directors-officers), D&O and EPLI are among the most commonly required policies for companies with formal governance structures and growing teams.

**Essential Policies (in addition to seed-stage coverage):**
- **Directors & Officers (D&O)** — protects the personal assets of founders, board members, and executives from lawsuits alleging mismanagement. Required by virtually all institutional investors. Most VC term sheets mandate D&O before the round can close.
- **Employment Practices Liability (EPLI)** — covers claims from employees related to wrongful termination, harassment, discrimination, and other employment-related issues. Becomes essential as headcount grows beyond the founding team.

**Typical Cost:** Expect your total annual premium to increase to **$10,000–$25,000 or more**. D&O insurance alone typically costs **$5,000–$10,000 per year** at this stage. EPLI adds another $1,500–$5,000 depending on headcount.

**What This Unlocks:** D&O coverage is a prerequisite for building the board and leadership team you need to scale. Experienced executives and independent board members will not join a company that exposes their personal assets to litigation. Having D&O in place before your Series A closes signals operational maturity and removes a common obstacle in investor due diligence. For a comprehensive guide on D&O, see [D&O Insurance for Startups: 5 Ways It Secures Your Future](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

### Growth Stage — Series B and Beyond ($30,000–$100,000+/year)

As a growth-stage company, you are a larger target with deeper pockets. Your operational complexity, contract values, customer count, headcount, and risk exposure have all magnified. Your insurance strategy must reflect this maturity.

**Essential Policies:**
- **Higher limits across all existing coverage lines** — often increasing to $5M or more per policy
- **Specialized coverage** depending on your operations — international liability, media liability, fiduciary liability, or product-specific endorsements
- **Umbrella/excess liability** — provides additional limits above your underlying policies for catastrophic scenarios

**Typical Cost:** Annual premiums range from **$30,000 to over $100,000**. Pricing becomes highly customized based on your specific revenue, industry vertical, claims history, geographic footprint, and risk management practices.

**What This Unlocks:** Robust, enterprise-grade insurance coverage becomes a key component of your risk management strategy and a signal of operational maturity to large customers, strategic partners, acquirers, and public market investors.

## Build Your Coverage Stack: The Policies That Matter Most

Understanding what each policy does is key to building the right protection without overspending. For tech startups, these four coverages are the pillars of a strong insurance program.

### Tech Errors & Omissions (E&O)

Technology Errors & Omissions insurance (Tech E&O) is a professional liability policy that protects your company if a client claims your software, product, or service failed to perform as promised and caused them a financial loss. Tech E&O is the single most important professional liability policy for any company that builds, sells, or licenses technology.

**Scenario:** A bug in your API causes a critical outage for your largest customer, resulting in significant lost revenue on their side. They sue your company to recover their financial losses. Tech E&O covers your legal defense costs and any potential settlement or judgment.

### Cyber Liability

Cyber liability insurance is a policy that covers costs arising from a data breach or cyber attack. For any startup handling user data — and especially for AI startups managing large datasets used in model training — cyber coverage is non-negotiable. This policy covers forensic investigation, customer notification, credit monitoring services, regulatory fines, and legal fees.

**Scenario:** A hacker breaches your cloud environment and exfiltrates a sensitive dataset used to train your language model. Your Cyber policy pays for the security experts needed to investigate and contain the breach, the legal costs of notifying all affected parties, and the PR response to manage reputational damage.

### Directors & Officers (D&O)

Directors & Officers insurance (D&O) is a management liability policy that protects the personal assets of your company's directors and officers — including founders — if they are sued for alleged wrongful acts performed in their leadership capacity. Investors require D&O as a condition of virtually every institutional funding round. For a complete guide on why D&O matters and the five specific ways it protects your company, see [D&O Insurance for Startups](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

**Scenario:** A group of early investors sues the board, alleging mismanagement of company funds following a difficult pivot. D&O insurance covers the legal defense costs for all founders and board members named personally in the lawsuit, plus any settlement.

### General Liability (CGL)

Commercial General Liability (CGL) is the foundational business insurance policy that covers third-party claims of bodily injury or property damage. While less tech-specific than E&O or Cyber, CGL is table stakes for signing an office lease, exhibiting at a trade show, or closing enterprise sales contracts that require proof of insurance.

## Lower Your Premiums With Smarter Risk Management

While costs increase as your company grows, you can take proactive steps to manage your premiums. Insurance carriers — including AI-powered carriers like Corgi — reward companies that demonstrate strong risk management practices, because those companies are statistically less likely to file claims.

| Strategy | Premium Impact | What to Do |
|---|---|---|
| **Implement Strong Cybersecurity** | Lowers Cyber premiums | Activate multi-factor authentication (MFA) everywhere, deploy endpoint detection and response (EDR) tools, conduct regular employee phishing simulations, and maintain an incident response plan. |
| **Maintain Clear Customer Contracts** | Lowers Tech E&O premiums | Work with legal counsel to ensure all customer contracts include clear scopes of work, limitation of liability clauses, and indemnification terms that cap your exposure. |
| **Document Internal Processes** | Lowers premiums broadly | Maintain formal, written policies for information security, employee conduct, data handling, and incident response. Underwriters view documentation as a signal of operational maturity. |
| **Bundle Your Policies** | Reduces total cost | Purchasing CGL, Tech E&O, and Cyber as a bundle from a single carrier is almost always cheaper than buying individual policies from separate providers. |
| **Choose a Full-Stack Carrier** | Eliminates middleman costs | A carrier like Corgi uses AI-powered underwriting for direct, risk-based pricing. There is no broker commission layer between you and your insurer, and AI-driven underwriting can price your specific risk more accurately than manual processes. |

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does startup insurance cost per month?

For a pre-seed or seed-stage startup, expect roughly **$170–$330 per month** for a core bundle of General Liability (CGL), Tech E&O, and Cyber Liability with $1M coverage limits. By Series A, with D&O and EPLI added, total monthly premiums typically run **$830–$2,080**. Most startup insurance policies are billed annually or quarterly rather than monthly.

### What is the cheapest startup insurance option?

The most cost-effective approach for early-stage startups is bundling General Liability, Tech E&O, and Cyber Liability into a single package from one carrier. This typically costs $2,000–$4,000/year. Avoid buying policies individually from different carriers, as bundling usually provides better rates and significantly simpler administration. Working with a direct carrier rather than a broker can also reduce costs by eliminating the broker commission layer.

### Does my startup need insurance before we have revenue?

Yes, if you are signing contracts of any kind. Customers, landlords, accelerator programs, and investors will require proof of insurance — specifically a Certificate of Insurance (COI) — regardless of your revenue. Having coverage in place before you need it prevents deal delays and ensures you can produce a COI instantly when a contract depends on it.

### When should I add D&O insurance to my startup's coverage?

You should have D&O insurance in place before your first institutional funding round closes. Most venture capital firms and institutional investors require D&O as a condition of investment, because it protects the personal assets of board members — including investor-appointed directors. Having D&O before you begin fundraising signals operational maturity and removes a common obstacle in due diligence. For a complete guide, see [D&O Insurance for Startups](do-insurance-for-startups.md).

### Is startup insurance tax deductible?

Yes. Business insurance premiums are generally tax-deductible as an ordinary business expense for your company. This includes premiums for CGL, Tech E&O, Cyber, D&O, and EPLI. Consult your accountant or tax advisor for specifics related to your company's structure and jurisdiction.

### What factors determine my startup insurance premium?

Insurance pricing for startups is based on several factors: your **annual revenue** (or projected revenue), **headcount**, **industry vertical** (AI/ML companies may face different risk profiles than SaaS), **coverage limits** selected, **deductible** amounts, **claims history**, and your **risk management practices** (cybersecurity posture, contract terms, internal documentation). AI-powered carriers like Corgi use these factors in automated underwriting models to generate more accurate, risk-based pricing.

### How does startup insurance pricing change as I raise more funding?

Each funding round typically increases your insurance costs for two reasons: your business has grown (more revenue, headcount, and contracts to protect), and investors require additional coverage types. Expect to add D&O and EPLI at Series A, increase limits at Series B, and potentially add specialized coverage at later stages. The cost increase is proportional to the growth in your risk profile — a company with 50 employees and $10M ARR has fundamentally different exposure than a 3-person pre-seed startup.
