# Fiduciary Liability Insurance for Startups: Protecting Employee Benefit Plan Administration (2026)

> Fiduciary liability insurance is a policy that protects your startup and the people managing your employee benefit plans — such as 401(k) plans, health insurance, HSAs, and FSAs — from claims alleging mistakes, errors, or breaches of fiduciary duty in administering those plans. For most startups, fiduciary liability becomes relevant once you offer retirement benefits and the company is making decisions about vendors, fees, investments, and plan administration.

**Key Takeaways:**

- Fiduciary liability insurance covers claims alleging that plan fiduciaries failed to act in the best interest of plan participants — including errors in enrollment, excessive fee allegations, imprudent investment selection, and vendor management failures.
- Fiduciary liability becomes relevant once your startup offers a 401(k), group health plan, HSA, or other employee benefit programs and is making decisions about plan vendors and administration.
- Fiduciary liability is **not** the same as D&O (management decisions), EPLI (employment disputes), or an ERISA fidelity bond (theft protection). Each covers a distinct category of risk.
- Even if you outsource plan administration to a third-party administrator (TPA), the company and its decision-makers can still be accused of making poor fiduciary choices — selecting the wrong vendor, failing to monitor fees, or providing inaccurate plan communications.
- Fiduciary claims can be expensive before liability is proven. Defense costs and settlement pressure rise quickly, making this coverage important even for companies that believe their plan governance is solid.

## What Is Fiduciary Liability Insurance?

Fiduciary liability insurance is a policy designed to cover claims that arise from the management and administration of employee benefit plans. These claims usually involve allegations that fiduciaries — the people responsible for making decisions about how benefit plans are run — failed to act in the best interest of plan participants.

In practice, fiduciary liability is about benefits governance. Under laws like [ERISA](https://www.dol.gov/general/topic/retirement/erisa) (the Employee Retirement Income Security Act), companies that sponsor benefit plans have specific obligations to plan participants, and the individuals making plan decisions can be held personally liable for breaches of fiduciary duty.

Fiduciary liability is distinct from other insurance policies:

| Policy | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Fiduciary Liability** | Employee benefit plan administration and governance claims |
| **[D&O](do-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Management decisions, investor claims, corporate governance |
| **[EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Employment disputes: discrimination, harassment, wrongful termination |
| **[Cyber](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Security incidents and privacy claims |
| **[CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Bodily injury and property damage |
| **[Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Customer claims about product/service failures |
| **ERISA Fidelity Bond** | Protects the *plan* against theft or dishonesty (not breach of duty) |

## Who Needs Fiduciary Liability Insurance

Fiduciary liability is most relevant for startups that:

- **Offer a 401(k) plan** or are launching one soon — the most common trigger
- **Provide group health benefits** and manage enrollment and eligibility decisions
- **Offer an HSA or FSA** as part of their benefits package
- **Use plan advisors, recordkeepers, and third-party administrators** — vendor selection creates fiduciary exposure
- **Are growing headcount** and adding benefits complexity
- **Have executives or HR leaders** making benefits decisions on behalf of employees

Even if you outsource administration, the company and its decision-makers can still be accused of making poor fiduciary choices — choosing the wrong vendor, failing to monitor fees, or providing inaccurate plan communications.

## What Fiduciary Liability Covers vs. What It Doesn't

### What Fiduciary Liability Typically Covers

Coverage varies by policy form, but fiduciary liability generally addresses allegations such as:

- **Failure to properly manage or administer a benefit plan** — errors in how the plan is run
- **Errors in enrollment, eligibility, or plan communications** — employees denied benefits or given incorrect information
- **Negligence in selecting or monitoring plan vendors** — choosing recordkeepers, TPAs, or advisors without adequate diligence
- **Excessive fee allegations** — claims that plan fees were not properly reviewed or were unreasonably high
- **Mismanagement of plan investments** — imprudent investment selection or failure to monitor fund performance (where applicable)
- **Defense costs for covered claims** — often the most significant benefit, as fiduciary litigation is expensive even before liability is determined

### What Fiduciary Liability Does Not Cover

- **Intentional wrongdoing, fraud, or personal profit** — not insurable
- **Prior known circumstances or claims** — must be new allegations
- **Benefits promised outside the plan terms** — depending on policy wording
- **Claims better addressed under other policies** — employment disputes belong under [EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md)
- **Taxes, penalties, or amounts uninsurable by law** — varies by jurisdiction and policy wording
- **Theft or dishonesty** — covered by an ERISA fidelity bond, not fiduciary liability

**Important:** Fiduciary liability is **not** the same as a fidelity bond. Many plans require a separate ERISA fidelity bond to protect the plan against theft or dishonesty. Fiduciary liability addresses claims alleging breach of duty in plan governance and administration — a fundamentally different risk.

## The Growing Risk of Fiduciary Claims

Fiduciary litigation has increased significantly in recent years, driven by greater awareness of plan fees, investment options, and fiduciary obligations. According to the [Government Accountability Office (GAO)](https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-21-357), excessive fee litigation against 401(k) plan sponsors has expanded beyond large corporations to include mid-sized and smaller plan sponsors.

| Trend | Impact for Startups |
|---|---|
| Excessive fee lawsuits expanding to smaller plans | Companies with 100+ participants increasingly targeted |
| Average 401(k) lawsuit settlement | $5–$15 million for large plans; defense costs alone $250K–$1M+ |
| DOL enforcement actions increasing | More audits and investigations of plan administration |
| Participant awareness growing | Employees are more likely to compare fees and performance |
| Vendor consolidation | Fewer vendor choices can create fiduciary scrutiny around selection process |

Even for startups with smaller plans, the defense cost exposure alone makes fiduciary liability coverage worthwhile. A DOL investigation or participant lawsuit can generate $100,000+ in legal fees before any liability determination.

## Common Fiduciary Claim Scenarios for Startups

These examples illustrate typical claim triggers (not promises of coverage):

- **Excessive 401(k) fees:** A group of employees alleges the 401(k) plan paid excessive recordkeeping fees that should have been negotiated down as the plan grew.
- **Imprudent investments:** Employees allege the plan investment lineup was imprudent or not properly monitored, resulting in poor returns compared to available alternatives.
- **Enrollment error:** An eligibility or enrollment error causes an employee to miss benefits coverage during a critical period, and they allege harm from the gap.
- **Inaccurate communications:** A plan communication contains inaccurate information about benefits, and employees claim they relied on it when making decisions.
- **Vendor failure:** A vendor mistake leads to plan administration issues — incorrect contributions, delayed enrollments, or data errors — and the company is drawn into the dispute as the plan sponsor.
- **Plan transition errors:** During a vendor migration (changing recordkeepers or TPAs), contributions are misrouted or blackout periods are mismanaged, and participants allege harm from the disruption.

## How to Think About Limits and Retention

Fiduciary liability usually involves two key numbers:

- **Limit:** Maximum the policy pays for covered loss
- **Retention:** What the company pays before coverage responds (often applies to the entity)

| Factor | Impact on Limit Selection |
|---|---|
| Size of benefit plan (assets under management) | Larger plans = larger potential exposure |
| Whether you have a 401(k) and its growth rate | 401(k) plans create the most fiduciary scrutiny |
| Complexity of vendors and investments | More decisions = more potential for allegations |
| Whether you have formal plan governance | Committees and documented processes reduce risk |
| Number of plan participants | More participants = more potential claimants |

**A practical approach:** Start with a baseline limit once you have a 401(k), and increase as plan assets and headcount grow.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is fiduciary liability insurance?

Fiduciary liability insurance is a policy that covers claims alleging mistakes or breaches of duty in administering employee benefit plans — such as a 401(k), health plan, HSA, or FSA. It protects both the company and the individuals making plan decisions (HR leaders, finance executives, plan committee members) from the costs of defending against and resolving fiduciary duty allegations.

### Do startups need fiduciary liability if they have a 401(k)?

Yes, in most cases. Once you offer a 401(k), you have fiduciary obligations around vendor selection, fee monitoring, investment lineup management, and plan administration. These decisions create exposure that fiduciary liability insurance is specifically designed to address. The cost of adding fiduciary liability is typically modest relative to the potential exposure.

### Is fiduciary liability the same as an ERISA bond?

No. An ERISA fidelity bond protects the *plan* against theft or dishonesty by plan fiduciaries. Fiduciary liability insurance covers allegations of *breach of fiduciary duty* — errors in administration, poor vendor selection, excessive fees, and imprudent investment decisions. Most plans that require a fidelity bond should also consider fiduciary liability insurance.

### Does fiduciary liability replace EPLI?

No. [EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md) covers employment disputes like discrimination, harassment, and wrongful termination. Fiduciary liability covers benefit plan governance and administration claims. These are separate risk categories that require separate policies. A wrongful termination lawsuit is an EPLI claim. An excessive 401(k) fee lawsuit is a fiduciary claim.

### When should a startup add fiduciary liability?

The most common trigger is launching a 401(k) plan, which creates immediate ERISA fiduciary obligations. Some startups also add fiduciary liability when they scale their health benefits, add an HSA or FSA, or when investor diligence requires a complete management liability stack.
