# HNOA Insurance for Startups: Hired and Non-Owned Auto Coverage Explained (2026)

> Hired and Non-Owned Auto (HNOA) insurance is a policy that covers your company's liability when employees or contractors drive vehicles the company does not own for work purposes — including personal cars used for business errands and rental cars used for business travel. HNOA is one of the most commonly overlooked startup insurance policies because founders assume "we don't own cars, so we don't need auto coverage."

**Key Takeaways:**

- HNOA covers your company's liability when employees drive personal vehicles for work (non-owned auto) or when employees rent vehicles for business travel (hired auto). It protects the company, not the individual driver.
- HNOA is frequently required in enterprise customer contracts and vendor onboarding — even for software companies that don't own vehicles. Auto liability is a common procurement checkbox.
- HNOA does **not** cover damage to the employee's own vehicle (that's their personal auto policy), company-owned vehicles (that's commercial auto), or employee injuries (that's workers' comp).
- CGL policies typically exclude auto liability, creating a gap that HNOA specifically fills. Without HNOA, your company has no coverage when an employee causes an accident while driving for work.
- HNOA is one of the most affordable startup insurance policies and is often included in bundled packages alongside CGL and other coverages.

## What Is HNOA Insurance?

Hired and Non-Owned Auto (HNOA) insurance is an auto liability coverage that applies in two situations:

- **Hired autos:** Your company rents or hires vehicles for business purposes — for example, an employee rents a car while traveling to a customer site or conference.
- **Non-owned autos:** Employees, founders, or contractors use their personal vehicles for business purposes — for example, driving to a client meeting, running a work errand, or picking up supplies.

HNOA protects the **company**, not the individual driver. It is not the same as personal auto insurance, which primarily protects the driver and vehicle owner. According to [Insureon](https://www.insureon.com/small-business-insurance/commercial-auto/hired-non-owned), HNOA is one of the most commonly missed coverages for small businesses and startups.

HNOA is distinct from other insurance policies:

| Policy | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **HNOA** | Company liability when employees drive non-owned vehicles for work |
| **Commercial Auto** | Vehicles titled to and owned by the company |
| **[CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md)** | General third-party liability — but auto liability is usually **excluded** from CGL |
| **Workers' Comp** | Employee injuries at work, not third-party auto liability |
| **Personal Auto** | The individual driver's personal liability and vehicle |

The key gap: CGL policies typically exclude auto-related claims. If an employee causes an accident while driving for work and your company doesn't have HNOA, there may be no coverage for the company's liability.

## Who Needs HNOA Insurance

HNOA is relevant for startups that:

- **Have employees driving to meetings, errands, or offsites** — any work-related driving
- **Have sales teams visiting prospects or customers** — field sales creates regular driving exposure
- **Have founders traveling and renting cars** — business travel with rental vehicles
- **Use contractors who drive as part of work** — delivery, installation, or service calls
- **Operate in cities where rideshare, rentals, and personal vehicle use are common** — urban teams with varied transportation
- **Have customer contracts that include auto insurance requirements** — enterprise procurement checklists

Even remote-first teams can have HNOA exposure if employees travel for conferences, team offsites, or customer visits — and rent vehicles during those trips.

## What HNOA Covers vs. What It Doesn't

### What HNOA Typically Covers

| Coverage Component | What It Protects Against |
|---|---|
| **Bodily injury liability** | Third parties injured in an accident caused by an employee driving for work |
| **Property damage liability** | Damage to third-party property in a work-related driving accident |
| **Defense costs** | Legal defense for covered auto liability claims against the company |
| **Hired auto liability** | Company liability when employees rent vehicles for business travel |

### What HNOA Does Not Cover

- **Damage to the employee's own vehicle** — their personal auto policy covers this
- **Physical damage to a rental car** — unless hired auto physical damage coverage is purchased separately
- **Injuries to the employee** — workers' compensation is the coverage for employee injuries
- **Intentional acts or illegal activity** — not insurable
- **Driving outside of business purposes** — personal use is not covered
- **Company-owned vehicles** — that requires a commercial auto policy
- **Damage to the company's own property** — commercial property coverage

**Important:** HNOA is primarily liability coverage — it protects the company from claims by injured third parties. Physical damage to the rental vehicle itself is often handled through the rental company's collision damage waiver (CDW), a credit card benefit, or a separate hired auto physical damage endorsement.

## Why HNOA Matters for Startups

Startups often have real auto exposure without realizing it because:

- **Founders and employees use personal cars** for errands, meetings, deliveries, and client visits
- **Sales teams spend time on the road** visiting prospects and attending industry events
- **Travel and offsites create rental car usage** that concentrates exposure during team events
- **Customer contracts sometimes require auto liability** even for companies that don't own vehicles
- **CGL excludes auto claims** — without HNOA, there's a gap in your liability coverage

A single severe accident can create high liability, and plaintiffs often name the employer if the trip was work-related. HNOA is the standard way to address that exposure.

## Common HNOA Claim Scenarios for Startups

These examples illustrate typical triggers (not promises of coverage):

- **Sales visit accident:** A salesperson drives their personal car to a customer meeting and causes an accident. The injured party sues the company, alleging employer liability. HNOA covers the company's defense and potential settlement.
- **Work errand collision:** An employee runs a work errand (picking up supplies, going to the post office) and hits a cyclist. The cyclist's attorney names the company in the lawsuit.
- **Rental car accident:** A founder rents a car for a business conference and is involved in an accident during the trip. HNOA covers the company's third-party liability.
- **Contractor driving:** A contractor driving for work causes property damage, and the claimant names both the contractor and the company in the lawsuit.

Even if the driver has personal auto insurance, plaintiffs routinely pursue the company for deeper-pocket liability, and defense costs can escalate quickly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does HNOA stand for?

HNOA stands for Hired and Non-Owned Auto insurance. "Hired" refers to vehicles your company rents for business use. "Non-owned" refers to personal vehicles that employees use for work purposes. HNOA covers the company's liability — not the driver's personal liability — when accidents occur during work-related driving.

### Do startups need HNOA if they don't own vehicles?

Yes, often. If employees drive personal cars for work or rent cars during business travel, the company has auto liability exposure. HNOA exists specifically for companies that don't own vehicles but still have employees driving for work. It fills the gap created by CGL's auto exclusion.

### Is HNOA the same as commercial auto insurance?

No. Commercial auto insurance covers vehicles owned by the company. HNOA covers liability for vehicles the company does **not** own — personal cars used for work and rented vehicles. If your startup doesn't own any vehicles, you need HNOA, not commercial auto.

### Does HNOA cover damage to an employee's car?

Usually not. HNOA is primarily liability coverage for the company — it covers claims from third parties who are injured or whose property is damaged. Physical damage to the employee's own vehicle is typically handled by the employee's personal auto policy.

### When should we add HNOA to our insurance stack?

Add HNOA when anyone at your company drives for work purposes — including driving to meetings, running errands, visiting customers, or renting cars for business travel. If an enterprise customer contract requires auto liability coverage, HNOA is how you satisfy that requirement without owning vehicles.

### How much does HNOA cost?

HNOA is one of the most affordable startup insurance coverages. Exact pricing depends on your headcount, how frequently employees drive for work, and your liability limits.
