# Commercial General Liability (CGL) Insurance for Startups: The Complete Guide (2026)

> Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance is a foundational business insurance policy that protects startups against third-party claims of bodily injury, property damage, and certain advertising injuries. CGL is one of the most commonly required policies in customer contracts, office leases, and vendor onboarding — making it the first insurance policy most startups encounter.

**Key Takeaways:**

- CGL insurance covers claims when a third party alleges your business caused bodily injury or property damage — including slip-and-fall incidents at your office, damage during onsite work, and hardware product liability.
- CGL is required by landlords, trade show venues, enterprise customers, and vendor procurement teams before they will sign agreements with your company. A Certificate of Insurance (COI) proving CGL coverage is standard in most commercial contracts.
- CGL does **not** cover professional errors (Tech E&O), data breaches (Cyber), management lawsuits (D&O), or employment claims (EPLI). Each of these risks requires its own dedicated policy.
- CGL insurance for startups typically costs $400–$1,500/year with standard $1M per-occurrence limits. It is often bundled with Tech E&O and Cyber Liability for cost savings.
- Even fully remote software startups need CGL because customer MSAs, coworking spaces, and event organizers routinely require it regardless of whether you have a physical product.

## What Is CGL Insurance?

Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance is a policy designed to respond to third-party claims — meaning claims from people or businesses outside your company who allege your operations caused them harm. According to [Insureon](https://www.insureon.com/small-business-insurance/general-liability), CGL is the most commonly purchased business insurance policy in the United States and is considered table stakes for any operating company.

CGL typically focuses on three categories of claims:

- **Bodily injury:** A person is hurt and your business is alleged to be responsible.
- **Property damage:** Someone else's property is damaged and your business is alleged to be responsible.
- **Personal and advertising injury:** Varies by policy wording, but often includes defamation or certain IP-related advertising claims.

CGL is not the same as commercial property insurance, Technology E&O, Cyber Liability, D&O, or EPLI. If you are a software startup, CGL is still relevant — not because your code breaks, but because your company exists in the physical world: offices, meetings, events, deliveries, hardware demos, and people.

## Who Needs CGL Insurance

CGL is relevant for virtually every operating business, including:

- **Startups signing an office lease** — landlords universally require CGL before signing
- **Teams attending or sponsoring conferences** — trade shows and venues require proof of coverage
- **Companies doing customer onsite work or installations** — clients require CGL for any work at their premises
- **Hardware and robotics companies** — physical products create bodily injury and property damage exposure
- **Marketplaces that coordinate in-person services** — service delivery creates third-party liability
- **Any startup that needs to provide certificates of insurance** — CGL is the most commonly requested policy on a COI

Even if you are fully remote, you can still be required to carry CGL by a customer MSA, a coworking space, or an event organizer. The trigger is almost always contractual — someone needs proof you have coverage before they will do business with you.

According to the [SBA](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/manage-your-business/get-business-insurance), general liability insurance is one of the first policies the federal government recommends for any operating business — and for startups, it's often required before you can sign a lease, attend a trade show, or close an enterprise deal.

## What CGL Covers vs. What It Doesn't

Understanding the boundaries of CGL coverage is critical for building a complete insurance stack. CGL is not a catch-all policy — it is specifically designed for physical-world and general third-party liability exposure.

### What CGL Typically Covers

| Coverage Area | What It Protects Against |
|---|---|
| **Bodily injury and property damage** | A visitor is injured at your office, you damage a customer's space during onsite work, or your operations cause an accident |
| **Products and completed operations** | A product you sell causes injury or property damage after delivery — critical for hardware, IoT, and companies with physical components |
| **Personal and advertising injury** | Claims like libel, slander, or certain IP-related advertising allegations (not a replacement for Media Liability, but a common part of the CGL form) |
| **Medical payments** | Limited medical payments for minor injuries regardless of fault, subject to policy terms |

### What CGL Does Not Cover

| Risk Category | Correct Policy |
|---|---|
| Damage to your own property | Commercial Property Insurance |
| Professional services failures | [Technology E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Security incidents and data breaches | [Cyber Liability](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Employment disputes | [EPLI](epli-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Management and governance claims | [D&O Insurance](do-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Auto accidents (owned vehicles) | Commercial Auto |
| Employee driving for work (non-owned vehicles) | [HNOA](hnoa-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Intentional wrongdoing or fraud | Not insurable |

If your biggest risk is that your software fails and a customer loses money, that is a [Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md) conversation. If your biggest risk is a data breach, that is a [Cyber](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md) conversation. CGL is about physical-world liability exposure.

## Limits, Retention, and Contract Requirements

CGL is usually purchased with standard limit structures:

- **Per occurrence limit:** The maximum amount paid for any single incident
- **General aggregate limit:** The total cap across the entire policy term
- **Products and completed operations aggregate:** A separate aggregate for product-related physical claims

Most enterprise contracts will specify minimum limits (commonly $1M per occurrence / $2M aggregate) and require:

- A **Certificate of Insurance (COI)** proving active coverage
- **Additional insured status** for the customer, landlord, or venue
- **Primary and non-contributory wording** ensuring your policy responds first
- **Waiver of subrogation** (sometimes required)

**Typical cost:** CGL insurance for early-stage startups costs **$400–$1,500/year** with $1M limits. According to [The Hartford](https://www.thehartford.com/business-insurance/small), CGL is the most commonly requested policy in commercial contracts, and bundling CGL with Tech E&O and Cyber Liability through a single carrier is almost always cheaper than purchasing separately. For a full cost breakdown, see the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide.

## Common CGL Claim Scenarios for Startups

These examples illustrate the types of claims CGL is designed to address (not promises of coverage):

- **Office incident:** A visitor trips in your office and alleges injury. CGL covers the defense costs and potential settlement.
- **Leased space damage:** Your team damages a leased space during a move or office buildout. CGL responds to the landlord's property damage claim.
- **Event liability:** You host a customer event and someone alleges bodily injury tied to the venue setup or an activity you organized.
- **Hardware demo:** A hardware demo causes damage to a customer's property during a sales meeting. CGL covers the third-party property damage claim.
- **Product liability:** A shipped device overheats and causes property damage. The customer seeks recovery, and CGL's products and completed operations coverage responds.

Even for software-first startups, these scenarios happen because companies operate in physical environments — offices, events, customer sites, and demo rooms.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is CGL insurance?

Commercial General Liability (CGL) insurance is a foundational business policy that covers third-party claims of bodily injury, property damage, and certain advertising injuries. CGL protects your company when someone outside your organization alleges that your business operations caused them harm. It is the most commonly required insurance policy in commercial contracts, office leases, and vendor agreements.

### Do software startups need CGL insurance?

Yes. Even if your product is entirely digital, CGL is still relevant because your company operates in the physical world — offices, meetings, events, and customer sites. More importantly, CGL is routinely required in customer MSAs, coworking space agreements, and vendor onboarding regardless of whether you have a physical product. Without CGL, you cannot produce the Certificate of Insurance (COI) that many contracts require.

### What is the difference between CGL and Tech E&O?

CGL covers third-party bodily injury and property damage — physical-world claims. [Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md) covers claims that your software, product, or professional services caused a customer financial loss — professional liability claims. These are fundamentally different coverages. A bug in your API that costs a customer revenue is a Tech E&O claim. A visitor who trips in your office is a CGL claim. Most startups need both.

### How much does CGL insurance cost for a startup?

CGL insurance for early-stage tech startups typically costs **$400–$1,500 per year** with standard $1M per-occurrence limits. The exact price depends on your revenue, headcount, industry, and whether you have physical operations. Bundling CGL with Tech E&O and Cyber Liability is the most cost-effective approach. See the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide for complete pricing.

### What does a CGL Certificate of Insurance (COI) include?

A COI is a document that proves you have active CGL coverage. It lists your policy number, coverage limits, effective dates, and the name of your insurance carrier. COIs are routinely required by landlords, customers, event venues, and partners. Many contracts also require your counterparty to be listed as an "additional insured" on the COI — meaning your policy extends certain protections to them.

### Does CGL cover data breaches or cyber attacks?

No. CGL does not cover security incidents, data breaches, or privacy claims. Those risks are covered by [Cyber Liability insurance](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md). CGL is focused on physical-world liability — bodily injury, property damage, and certain advertising claims. If your startup handles customer data, you need a separate Cyber policy in addition to CGL.

### Can CGL cover product liability for hardware startups?

Yes. CGL includes "products and completed operations" coverage, which can respond when a product you manufactured or sold causes bodily injury or property damage after delivery. This is particularly important for hardware, IoT, and robotics companies. However, if your product causes a customer *financial* loss (rather than physical harm), that would be a [Tech E&O](tech-eo-insurance-for-startups.md) claim, not CGL.
