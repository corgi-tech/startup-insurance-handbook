# Tech E&O Insurance for Startups: Errors & Omissions Coverage Explained (2026)

> Technology Errors & Omissions (Tech E&O) insurance, also called Professional Liability insurance, is a policy that covers claims alleging your software, product, or professional services caused a customer financial loss. Tech E&O is the most important professional liability policy for any company that builds, sells, or licenses technology — and is routinely required in enterprise procurement before contracts can be signed.

**Key Takeaways:**

- Tech E&O covers defense costs, settlements, and judgments when a customer alleges your technology product or services failed to perform and caused them financial harm — including outages, integration failures, implementation errors, and AI output failures.
- Enterprise customers require Tech E&O by name during vendor onboarding. Procurement teams will not sign MSAs, SOWs, or SLAs without a Certificate of Insurance (COI) showing E&O coverage.
- Tech E&O is a **claims-made** policy, meaning you must report claims during the active policy period. Letting coverage lapse can create a dangerous gap where past work is uninsured.
- Tech E&O is not the same as Cyber Liability (security incidents), CGL (bodily injury), or D&O (management decisions). Each covers a distinct category of risk.
- Tech E&O for startups typically costs $1,000–$3,000/year at seed stage with $1M limits, often bundled with CGL and Cyber Liability.

## What Is Technology E&O Insurance?

Technology Errors & Omissions (Tech E&O) insurance is a professional liability policy that covers your defense costs and damages when a customer alleges your software, SaaS platform, API, AI product, or professional services failed to perform and caused them a financial loss. According to [Insureon](https://www.insureon.com/small-business-insurance/errors-and-omissions), E&O insurance is one of the most commonly required policies for technology companies and professional service providers.

Tech E&O is fundamentally different from:

| Policy | What It Covers |
|---|---|
| **Tech E&O** | Customer financial loss from your product/service failure |
| **[CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Bodily injury and property damage to third parties |
| **[Cyber Liability](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Security incidents, data breaches, and privacy claims |
| **[D&O](do-insurance-for-startups.md)** | Management decisions and investor/shareholder claims |

Tech E&O protects your revenue when your software or AI services fail to perform. It is the policy that stands between a product failure and a lawsuit that drains your cash reserves.

## Who Needs Tech E&O Insurance

Tech E&O is most commonly required by:

- **SaaS companies selling B2B subscriptions** — enterprise customers require E&O in vendor agreements
- **AI companies providing models, agents, or decision tools** — AI outputs that cause customer harm trigger E&O claims
- **Developer platforms, APIs, and infrastructure** — downstream customers rely on your tooling for critical operations
- **Fintech and payments software** — financial transaction failures create significant exposure (often paired with Cyber)
- **Marketplaces that provide services, workflows, or outcomes** — service delivery creates professional liability
- **Startups handling implementation, onboarding, or professional services** — project delivery disputes are classic E&O claims

If customers rely on your product to run operations, route money, make decisions, or meet compliance requirements, you need Tech E&O insurance.

## What Tech E&O Covers vs. What It Doesn't

### What Tech E&O Typically Covers

Coverage depends on the policy wording, but Tech E&O is designed around claims alleging:

- **Failure of your software or platform to perform as intended** — bugs, outages, or functional failures
- **Errors in professional services, configuration, or implementation** — mistakes during onboarding or deployment
- **Missed deadlines tied to project delivery** — when tied to covered allegations of professional negligence
- **Negligence allegations tied to your product or services** — claims you fell below professional standards
- **Customer reliance on your outputs, reports, or recommendations** — AI-generated outputs that cause harm
- **Defense costs for covered claims** — often the largest single cost, even when the claim lacks merit

### What Tech E&O Does Not Cover

| Exclusion | Why |
|---|---|
| Intentional wrongdoing, fraud, or dishonesty | Not insurable |
| Known issues or prior claims | Must be reported during the policy period when they arise |
| Pure breach of contract (not tied to negligence) | Contract disputes without professional liability allegations |
| Bodily injury and property damage | Covered by [CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) |
| Security incidents and privacy claims | Covered by [Cyber](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md) (though overlap can exist) |
| Fines and penalties uninsurable by law | Regulatory in nature |
| Warranty guarantees beyond professional standards | Overly broad contractual commitments |

**Important:** If your MSA promises unlimited liability or broad warranties, it creates gaps no policy can fix. Aligning your contract terms with your insurance limits is critical to ensuring your startup remains protected.

## How Limits and Retention Work

When choosing Tech E&O coverage, founders focus on two numbers:

- **Limit:** The maximum the policy pays for covered loss (defense + indemnity)
- **Retention:** The amount you pay before coverage responds (similar to a deductible)

| Factor | Impact on Limit Selection |
|---|---|
| Enterprise customer minimums | Contracts often specify $1M–$5M E&O limits |
| Revenue concentration | Higher dependency on a single customer increases exposure |
| SLAs and uptime commitments | Performance guarantees increase potential claim severity |
| Indemnification clauses | Broad indemnities in contracts amplify financial exposure |
| Implementation services | Hands-on project work creates additional professional liability |

**A practical approach:** Use your largest customer's requirements as a baseline limit, then add headroom for defense costs.

**Claims-made coverage:** Tech E&O is typically written on a claims-made basis, meaning you must report any claims during the active policy period. Maintaining continuous coverage is critical — letting a claims-made policy lapse means past work may become uninsured.

## Common Tech E&O Claim Scenarios for Startups

These examples map to why companies buy Tech E&O (not promises of coverage):

- **Integration failure:** A customer alleges an API integration failed and caused invoicing errors, leading to financial loss. Tech E&O covers defense costs and potential settlement.
- **Deployment issues:** A deployment misses requirements and the customer claims rework costs and project delays. E&O responds to the negligence allegation.
- **Outage impact:** An outage leads to business interruption for a customer who alleges your platform caused their revenue loss. Defense costs alone can be substantial.
- **Product bug:** A product bug creates incorrect outputs and the customer alleges reliance damages — they made business decisions based on faulty data from your system.
- **Services overrun:** A services project runs over scope and the customer alleges negligence in project management and delivery.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does E&O stand for?

E&O stands for Errors and Omissions. In the technology context, it is usually called Technology E&O or Tech E&O. It is a professional liability policy that covers claims alleging your software, product, or services caused a customer financial loss due to errors, omissions, or failure to perform.

### Is Tech E&O the same as cyber insurance?

No. [Cyber insurance](cyber-insurance-for-startups.md) covers security incidents, data breaches, and privacy claims — the costs of responding to a hack or data exposure. Tech E&O covers performance, delivery, and professional services disputes — claims that your product failed and cost a customer money. A data breach is a cyber claim. A software outage that causes customer revenue loss is a Tech E&O claim. Most startups need both policies.

### Do SaaS companies need E&O insurance?

Yes. Enterprise customers routinely require E&O insurance during vendor onboarding. If you sell B2B software with SLAs, handle customer data, or provide implementation services, Tech E&O is a core policy. Without it, you cannot close most enterprise contracts.

### How much does Tech E&O cost for a startup?

Tech E&O for seed-stage startups typically costs **$1,000–$3,000 per year** with $1M limits when bundled with CGL and Cyber. Pricing depends on revenue, customer profile, contract terms, and coverage limits. At Series A and beyond, limits typically increase as enterprise customer requirements grow. See the [Startup Insurance Cost by Funding Stage](startup-insurance-cost-by-funding-stage.md) guide for complete pricing.

### What is the difference between Tech E&O and CGL?

[CGL](cgl-insurance-for-startups.md) covers third-party bodily injury and property damage — physical-world claims. Tech E&O covers financial loss claims tied to your product or professional services performance. A visitor who trips in your office is a CGL claim. A software bug that costs a customer revenue is a Tech E&O claim. Most tech startups need both.

### What happens if I let my Tech E&O policy lapse?

Tech E&O is a claims-made policy, which means claims must be reported during the active policy period. If you let your policy lapse, you lose the ability to report claims for work you performed while the policy was active. This creates a dangerous uninsured gap. Maintaining continuous, uninterrupted Tech E&O coverage is critical for any company with ongoing customer relationships.

### Does Tech E&O cover AI-related claims?

Tech E&O can cover claims arising from AI product failures — for example, if an AI model generates incorrect outputs that a customer relies on and suffers financial harm. However, not all policies are designed with AI risk in mind. Generic Tech E&O forms may not adequately cover risks like algorithmic bias, hallucinations, or training data IP disputes.
